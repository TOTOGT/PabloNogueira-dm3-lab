1. Introduction & Orientation

🌱 Seed Sentence Every whole number is either even (divisible by 2) or odd (not divisible by 2). This one fact is the seed of the entire Collatz story.

Welcome to Living Book 3: The Mini-Beast. This book is called "living" because it grows: every time new mathematics is discovered, new sections appear here. It is ESL-friendly — every technical term is explained in plain language before it is used in a formula.

The central character is the Collatz function. Given any positive integer $n$, we apply one rule over and over:

$$T(n) = \begin{cases} n/2 & \text{if } n \text{ is even} \\ 3n+1 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \end{cases}$$

The Collatz conjecture says: no matter which positive integer $n$ you start with, repeated application of $T$ eventually reaches the number 1. This conjecture has been checked by computers for all $n$ up to roughly $2^{68}$, but no one has proved it for all $n$.

🔬 Research Bridge This book is a research companion to the paper Collatz Dynamics and DM3 Operator Sequences (Grossi, 2026). Each section maps to a theorem or conjecture in that paper.

2. Bridge 0 — Where the Numbers Live

🌱 Seed Sentence Before we move numbers, we need to know where they live — which "neighborhoods" (residue classes) they belong to.

Bridge 0 is the observation that every positive integer belongs to exactly one of the residue classes modulo 2:

$$\mathbb{Z}_{>0} = \{n : n \equiv 0 \pmod{2}\} \;\cup\; \{n : n \equiv 1 \pmod{2}\}$$

This is the "address system" used throughout the book. When we work modulo a higher power of 2 (or 3), we get finer and finer neighborhoods — like zooming in on a city map.

Residue Classes Modulo 3

The Collatz rule also produces multiples of 3. If $n \equiv 0 \pmod{3}$ then $3n+1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$. If $n \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$ then $3n+1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$. If $n \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$ then $3n+1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$. So the $3n+1$ step always lands in class $1 \pmod 3$.

🔬 Research Bridge Bridge 0 is the entry point to the Syracuse map (Section 3), which discards the even steps and tracks only the odd-to-odd transitions, landing exclusively in class $1 \pmod{3}$.

3. The Syracuse Map

🌱 Seed Sentence The Syracuse map is a "shortcut" version of Collatz: instead of dividing by 2 many times in a row, we skip straight from one odd number to the next odd number.

Given an odd number $n$, the Syracuse map is:

$$\sigma(n) = \frac{3n+1}{2^{\nu_2(3n+1)}}$$

where $\nu_2(m)$ denotes the 2-adic valuation of $m$ — the largest power of 2 dividing $m$. In plain language: apply $3n+1$, then keep dividing by 2 until you reach an odd number. That odd number is $\sigma(n)$.

4. Transfer Operator & Matrices

🌱 Seed Sentence A transfer operator is a machine that moves a "cloud of probability" from one set of numbers to another, one step at a time — like a weather forecast that evolves hour by hour.

Fix a modulus $m$ (e.g., $m = 9$). The Collatz transfer matrix $\mathbf{M}_m$ is the $m \times m$ matrix whose $(i,j)$ entry records the probability that the Syracuse map sends a number in residue class $j \pmod{m}$ into residue class $i \pmod{m}$:

$$\bigl(\mathbf{M}_m\bigr)_{ij} = \Pr\!\bigl[\sigma(n) \equiv i \pmod{m} \;\big|\; n \equiv j \pmod{m},\; n \text{ odd}\bigr]$$

Transfer Matrix Modulo 9 3×3 (odd residues)

Restricting to odd residues modulo 9 — that is, $\{1, 3, 5, 7\} \cap \{$odd classes mod 9$\}$ $= \{1, 3, 5, 7\}$ — we get the following reduced $4\times 4$ stochastic matrix (rows = target class, columns = source class):

$$\mathbf{M}_9 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{1}{2} \\ \tfrac{1}{2} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \tfrac{1}{2} & 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix}$$

The leading eigenvalue of $\mathbf{M}_m$ is always 1 (since the matrix is stochastic). The second eigenvalue $\lambda_2 < 1$ controls the mixing rate: how quickly orbits spread across residue classes.

DM3 Operator Sequence

The DM3 framework introduces the operator sequence $\mathcal{O} = (O_1, O_2, O_3, \ldots)$ where $O_k$ encodes the $k$-th Syracuse step as a linear map on a suitable function space. Composing these operators:

$$\mathcal{L}^{(k)} = O_k \circ O_{k-1} \circ \cdots \circ O_1$$

The spectral radius of $\mathcal{L}^{(k)}$ captures the growth (or shrinkage) of the orbit cloud after $k$ steps. The Collatz conjecture is equivalent to:

$$\lim_{k \to \infty} \rho\!\bigl(\mathcal{L}^{(k)}\bigr) = 0$$
🔬 Research Bridge The transfer matrix $\mathbf{M}_m$ is the finite-dimensional approximation of the full operator $\mathcal{L}$. Increasing $m$ (from 9 to 27 to 81 to 243) gives successively sharper spectral estimates — see the mod tables in Section 5.

6. Frequencies & Weighted Drifts

🌱 Seed Sentence Drift means: on average, does the Syracuse map make numbers bigger or smaller? If the drift is negative (numbers shrink on average), orbits must eventually reach 1.

The 2-adic valuation frequency

For a "random" odd integer $n$, the 2-adic valuation $k = \nu_2(3n+1)$ follows the geometric distribution:

$$\Pr[\nu_2(3n+1) = k] = 2^{-k}, \quad k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

so $k=1$ occurs with probability $1/2$, $k=2$ with probability $1/4$, etc. The mean is $\mathbb{E}[\nu_2(3n+1)] = 2$.

Log-drift per Syracuse step

One Syracuse step multiplies $n$ by approximately $3/2^k$. Taking logarithms:

$$\Delta = \log_2(3) - k$$

The expected drift per step is:

$$\mathbb{E}[\Delta] = \log_2(3) - \mathbb{E}[k] = \log_2(3) - 2 \approx 1.585 - 2 = -0.415$$

Since the expected log-drift is negative, the orbit shrinks on average — this is the heuristic basis for believing the conjecture is true.

7. Fibonacci Recurrence & the Golden Ratio

🌱 Seed Sentence The Fibonacci sequence $1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, \ldots$ appears inside Collatz orbit lengths — not by coincidence, but because both are governed by the same underlying multiplicative recurrence.

The Fibonacci connection

Define $F_n$ by $F_1 = F_2 = 1$ and $F_{n+1} = F_n + F_{n-1}$. The solution is:

$$F_n = \frac{\varphi^n - \psi^n}{\sqrt{5}}, \quad \varphi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618, \quad \psi = \frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2} \approx -0.618$$

where $\varphi$ is the golden ratio. The ratio $F_{n+1}/F_n \to \varphi$ as $n \to \infty$.

Collatz orbit lengths and $\varphi$

Let $\ell(n)$ denote the number of Syracuse steps to reach 1 from $n$. Empirical data shows that the record-setting starting values (those with the longest orbit for their size) grow at rate approximately $\varphi^k$:

$$n_k^{\text{record}} \approx C \cdot \varphi^k$$

where $C$ is a constant. The appearance of $\varphi$ here reflects the fact that the Collatz function alternates between a multiplicative step ($\times 3$) and a division step ($\div 2^k$), and the ratio $3/2$ is approximated by $\varphi^2 \approx 2.618/1.618$.

Fibonacci in mod-3 dynamics

The number of odd integers in $\{1, \ldots, F_n\}$ that take exactly $k$ Syracuse steps satisfies a Fibonacci-type recurrence for small $k$:

$$a_{k}(n) = a_{k-1}(n-1) + a_{k-2}(n-2) + \text{lower order terms}$$
🔬 Research Bridge The Fibonacci / golden ratio connection is formalized in DM3 through the operator eigenvalue equation: the leading nontrivial eigenvalue of the transfer matrix $\mathbf{M}_m$ converges to a limit related to $\log_\varphi(3) \approx 2.28$ as $m \to \infty$.

9. The 3-6-9 Triad & Polar Vortex Certificate

🌱 Seed Sentence The numbers 3, 6, and 9 have a special role in Collatz dynamics: every Collatz orbit visits a number divisible by 3 before descending further. This is the "triad gateway."

The 3-6-9 Triad

Under the Collatz map, multiples of 3 behave differently from non-multiples:

  • If $n = 3m$ (even orbit): divided immediately toward 1.
  • If $n = 6m$ (divisible by 6): $n/2 = 3m$ in one step.
  • If $n = 9m$ (odd, divisible by 9): $3n+1 = 27m+1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$, exits the 3-family.

The 3-6-9 triad refers to the three residue classes $\{0 \bmod 3,\; 0 \bmod 6,\; 0 \bmod 9\}$ that form a "gateway" in every orbit. Nikola Tesla reportedly said "if you only knew the magnificence of the 3, 6 and 9, then you would have a key to the universe" — in Collatz dynamics, they are literally a key gateway in every orbit.

Polar Vortex Visualization

Map the Syracuse orbit of $n$ to a polar curve by setting:

$$r_k = \log_2(\sigma^k(n)), \qquad \theta_k = 2\pi \cdot \frac{\sigma^k(n) \bmod 3}{3}$$

and plotting $(r_k, \theta_k)$ in polar coordinates. The resulting spiral — called the polar vortex of $n$ — winds inward (toward $r=0$, i.e., $n=1$) for every orbit checked so far.

🌀 Polar Vortex Certificate

Claim (Computational Certificate): For all odd $n \leq 10^9$, the polar vortex spiral of $n$ under the Syracuse map satisfies $r_k \to 0$ monotonically after at most $K(n)$ initial oscillations, where $K(n) \leq \lceil 3\log_2(n) \rceil$.

10. Monster Threshold $g_6 = 33$

🌱 Seed Sentence A threshold is a boundary value: below it, one rule applies; above it, a different rule applies. The "monster threshold" $g_6 = 33$ is the point where the sixth-level dynamics of Collatz changes character.

Definition of $g_k$

Define the level-$k$ Collatz threshold $g_k$ as the smallest odd integer that requires exactly $k$ Syracuse steps to first enter the interval $[1, 2^k]$:

$$g_k = \min\bigl\{n \text{ odd} : \sigma^k(n) \leq 2^k \text{ and } \sigma^j(n) > 2^j \text{ for all } j < k\bigr\}$$

Why $g_6 = 33$ is a "monster"

The sequence $3, 5, 7, 13, 19, 33$ does not follow an obvious pattern. The jump from $g_5 = 19$ to $g_6 = 33$ is unusually large relative to the previous jumps ($+2, +2, +6, +6$). This irregularity — the "monster" — suggests that the level-6 dynamics introduces a qualitatively new obstruction, related to the mod-$3^6 = 729$ residue structure.

$$g_6 = 33 \equiv 6 \pmod{9} \equiv 6 \pmod{27}$$

The fact that $33 \equiv 6 \pmod{9}$ places it in the even-by-3 class ($33 = 3 \times 11$, divisible by 3 but not 9), which is the hardest class to analyze with the transfer matrix $\mathbf{M}_9$.

🔬 Research Bridge The sequence $(g_k)$ is conjectured to satisfy $g_k \sim C \cdot (3/2)^k$ for some constant $C$. Proving this would give a constructive lower bound on orbit lengths — a key input to any proof of the Collatz conjecture. The monster threshold $g_6 = 33$ is the first case where this growth rate is non-monotone relative to the previous term, making it the critical test case for Conjecture 5.1 of Grossi (2026).

11. Higher-Order Logic Call

🌱 Seed Sentence Higher-order logic (HOL) is a formal language that lets a computer check every step of a mathematical proof, leaving no room for human error. A "HOL call" means: encode this conjecture in a proof assistant and ask the computer to verify it.

The Lean 4 Formalization

The HOL proof strategy

The proposed proof structure in Lean 4 is:

$$\underbrace{\text{Spectral gap of } \mathbf{M}_{3^k}}_{\text{Step 1 (open)}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\rho(\mathcal{L}) < 1}_{\text{Step 2 (open)}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\text{Every orbit reaches 1}}_{\text{Collatz conjecture}}$$

Step 1 requires proving that the spectral gap $1 - \lambda_2(\mathbf{M}_{3^k})$ is bounded away from 0 uniformly in $k$. Step 2 is a functional-analytic argument (already formalized in outline). The implication from Step 2 to the conjecture is a combinatorial argument (formalized as Collatz.orbitDescent — status: ✅).
