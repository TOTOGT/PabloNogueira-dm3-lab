What Is the Mini-Beast?

The Mini-Beast is the informal name for the Collatz conjecture — one of the simplest rules in mathematics that no one has yet proved. Pick any positive whole number. If it is even, divide it by 2. If it is odd, multiply it by 3 and add 1. Repeat. The conjecture says you will always reach 1.

ESL Seed · A2 "We pick a number. We follow the rule. We always get to 1."
CEFR Note · B1
The word conjecture means a strong guess that has not been proved. Scientists and mathematicians use it when evidence points one way but no one has given a full logical proof yet.

Within the DM³ framework, the Mini-Beast acts as a stress-test for the Coherence Bridge: a minimal dynamical system whose long-range behaviour encodes deep arithmetic structure. The sections below build from first contact to research frontier.

Section 1

Bridge 0 — First Contact with the Rule

Bridge 0 is the entry point: you understand nothing yet except how to apply the rule by hand. That is enough to observe the Mini-Beast in action.

ESL Seed · A2 "Start at 6. Is 6 even? Yes. Divide: 6 ÷ 2 = 3. Is 3 odd? Yes. Multiply and add: 3 × 3 + 1 = 10. Continue …"
Collatz rule (formal):

T(n) = n / 2 if n ≡ 0 (mod 2)
T(n) = 3n + 1 if n ≡ 1 (mod 2)

Example — seed 27:
27 → 82 → 41 → 124 → 62 → 31 → 94 → 47 → … → 1
(111 steps — one of the longest journeys below 100)
CEFR Note · B2
The notation n ≡ 0 (mod 2) means "n leaves remainder 0 when divided by 2" — i.e., n is even. n ≡ 1 (mod 2) means n is odd.
Research Bridge

The stopping time of a seed n is the number of steps before reaching 1. Proving that the stopping time is finite for all positive integers is the open conjecture. As of 2024, all integers up to approximately 2.95 × 10²⁰ have been verified computationally (Oliveira e Silva, 2021; extended checks ongoing).

Write out each step on paper:

27 (odd) → 82
82 (even) → 41
41 (odd) → 124
124 (even)→ 62

31 (odd) → 94
94 (even) → 47
47 (odd) → 142
… (keep going) …
→ 4 → 2 → 1 ✓

Count how many steps you take. You should reach 111 before the sequence hits 1.

Section 2

Drifts — The Tendency to Fall

A drift in the Collatz context measures the average upward or downward movement per step. Because odd steps multiply by roughly 3 and even steps divide by 2, the long-run drift is negative — the sequence tends to fall toward 1.

ESL Seed · B1 "Every 'odd' step makes the number bigger. Every 'even' step makes it smaller. On average, the number gets smaller. That is the drift."
Expected drift per step (logarithmic):

E[log₂(T(n)/n)] = (1/2)·log₂(1/2) + (1/2)·log₂(3/2)
≈ (1/2)(−1) + (1/2)(0.585)
≈ −0.2075

Each step shrinks the average by about 20 % in log-scale.
CEFR Note · C1
This probabilistic argument assumes that parity (odd/even) behaves like a fair coin toss — an assumption that is heuristically reasonable but not proved in general. The DM³ drift operator formalises this as a signed coherence flux across scales.

Press "Run ▶" to see the sequence and step count.
Research Bridge

The negative drift is the basis of most probabilistic proofs of almost-all results: almost every integer eventually reaches 1. The exceptional set (if it exists) would consist of seed values that escape to infinity or enter a cycle other than 4→2→1. No such values have been found; their non-existence is the conjecture.

Section 3

Matrices — Encoding the Rule

The Collatz map can be expressed as a pair of 2×2 matrices acting on a two-component vector. This matrix encoding connects the Mini-Beast to linear algebra and, ultimately, to the DM³ operator chain.

ESL Seed · B2 "A matrix is a grid of numbers. When we multiply it with our starting vector, we get the next step in the sequence."
Matrix representation:

Even step: M_even = [1/2 0] applied to [n]
[0 1] [1]

Odd step: M_odd = [3 1] applied to [n]
[0 1] [1]

A trajectory of length k is: (M_σₖ · … · M_σ₁) · [n, 1]ᵀ
where σᵢ ∈ {even, odd} is the parity of step i.
CEFR Note · C1
The second component of the vector stays 1 throughout; it is a homogeneous coordinate that lets us encode the "+1" in the odd rule as a matrix multiplication rather than an addition. This is the same trick used in computer graphics transformations.
Seed n = 3 (odd): M_odd · [3,1]ᵀ = [3·3+1, 1]ᵀ = [10, 1]ᵀ
n = 10 (even): M_even· [10,1]ᵀ = [5, 1]ᵀ
n = 5 (odd): M_odd · [5,1]ᵀ = [16, 1]ᵀ
n = 16 (even×4): → 8 → 4 → 2 → 1 ✓
Research Bridge

The product of these matrices along a trajectory defines a Collatz word. The DM³ framework interprets this word as an operator-chain element in the G-series hierarchy (Grossi 2026, §3.2). Bounding the spectral radius of random matrix products is one active research direction (Lagarias & Kontorovich, 2010).

Section 4

Fibonacci & the Golden Ratio — Hidden Symmetry

The Fibonacci sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, …) and the Collatz sequence share a structural kinship: both are defined by simple local rules, yet produce complex global patterns. The golden ratio φ appears at the boundary of both.

ESL Seed · B1 "The Fibonacci numbers grow by adding the two previous numbers. The golden ratio is the number they grow toward, in a ratio: φ ≈ 1.618."
Fibonacci recurrence: F(n) = F(n−1) + F(n−2), F(1)=F(2)=1

Golden ratio: φ = (1 + √5) / 2 ≈ 1.61803…

Binet's formula: F(n) = (φⁿ − ψⁿ) / √5
where ψ = (1 − √5)/2 ≈ −0.618

Collatz–Fibonacci link:
The odd-step multiplier 3 and the even-step divisor 2 satisfy:
log_φ(3) ≈ 2.285, log_φ(2) ≈ 1.440
Their ratio ≈ 1.587 ≈ φ + 1/φ² (approximate resonance)
CEFR Note · C1
This resonance is not a theorem — it is a structural observation that guides DM³ model-building. The golden ratio appears in the Collatz literature as an exponent bound in the "Syracuse" compression (see Terras 1976). The DM³ framework explores whether φ encodes a deeper coherence symmetry at the drift boundary.
Research Bridge

Terras (1976) showed that the reduced Collatz map — which skips even steps — has a natural connection to the golden ratio through the distribution of odd iterates. Grossi (2026, §4.1) proposes that the DM³ coherence bridge is indexed by φ as a fixed-point attractor in the drift-normalized operator space.

Section 5

The 3-6-9 Triad — Arithmetic Structure

Tesla's famous observation that "if you knew the magnificence of 3, 6, and 9, you would have a key to the universe" resonates with real arithmetic: in base 10, all multiples of 3 reduce to 3, 6, or 9 by digital root. In the Collatz context, odd numbers divisible by 3 behave differently from other odds.

ESL Seed · B1 "If you add the digits of a multiple of 3, you get 3, 6, or 9. For example: 12 → 1+2 = 3. 24 → 2+4 = 6. 27 → 2+7 = 9."
Digital root (base 10):
dr(n) = 1 + ((n−1) mod 9) for n ≥ 1

Collatz mod-3 behaviour:
If n ≡ 0 (mod 3): T(n) is odd and ≡ 1 (mod 3) if n odd
If n ≡ 1 (mod 3): T(n) ≡ 1 (mod 3) if n odd (3n+1 ≡ 1)
If n ≡ 2 (mod 3): T(n) ≡ 2 (mod 3) if n odd (3n+1 ≡ 1... wait)

Key fact: 3n+1 is NEVER divisible by 3 when n is odd.
So multiples of 3 can only appear after an even-step halving.
CEFR Note · B2
This means the Collatz orbit "escapes" multiples of 3 at odd steps. In DM³ terms, the 3-6-9 triad forms an arithmetic invariant subspace that constrains the drift pattern. The triad is not mystical — it reflects modular arithmetic over the ring ℤ/9ℤ.

The residue structure of Collatz orbits modulo powers of 3 is studied in the context of 3-adic analysis (Bernstein 1994; Lagarias 2010). The DM³ 3-6-9 triad hypothesis proposes that coherence thresholds cluster near triadic resonance nodes in the operator hierarchy.

Section 6

Polar Vortex — Visualising the Orbit

Plotting each value in a Collatz orbit as a point on a spiral — with angle proportional to step number and radius proportional to value — reveals a polar vortex pattern. The orbit spirals inward, occasionally spiking outward, then converging.

ESL Seed · B1 "We draw each number as a point on a circle. The angle shows the step number. The distance from the centre shows the size of the number. We watch the spiral."

Angle = step index × 30°. Radius = log₂(value) × 14. Gold dot = origin (n=1).
